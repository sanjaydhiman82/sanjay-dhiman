My First Day at ETV JPMC Office — Bangalore, Tower D

Walking into one of Bangalore's most iconic tech campuses for the first time — nerves, wonder, and a flood of first impressions.

Sanjay Dhiman • April 27, 2026 • 6 min read

There are certain days you know will stay with you — days where the ordinary fabric of routine breaks open and something new rushes in. My first day at the ETV JPMC office in Bangalore, Tower D, was exactly that kind of day. I had been preparing for it mentally for weeks: ironing out every wrinkle, rehearsing what I'd say, mapping the route on Google Maps twice over. But nothing quite prepares you for the moment you actually walk through those doors.

The Commute — Bangalore on a Monday Morning

Anyone who has spent time in Bangalore knows that the city has its own rhythm — chaotic yet somehow musical. I left early, not wanting to trust the city's famously unpredictable traffic. The auto ride from my place to the office campus was a sensory experience in itself: the smell of filter coffee drifting from a darshini, the sound of school kids laughing on a bus, the blur of Bangalore's green canopy flashing past.

The campus entrance appeared before I had fully processed that I was here. A clean, well-marked gate, security personnel in crisp uniforms, and a long tree-lined path leading inward. Even from the entrance, Tower D had a presence — glass and steel catching the early morning light, the kind of building that makes you straighten your posture without thinking.

First Steps Inside Tower D

The lobby was everything I'd imagined and more. High ceilings, polished floors, the quiet hum of air conditioning, and that unmistakable energy of a place where decisions happen. There were people already deep in conversation near the coffee station. Badges clipped to lanyards, laptops tucked under arms, a casual confidence in how everyone moved. I was definitely the newest person in the room.

💡 First-day tip: Arrive 15 minutes early. Not to show off punctuality — but to give yourself a quiet moment to absorb the space before the day begins in earnest.

The onboarding desk was easy to find. A friendly HR executive handed me a welcome kit — badge, access card, some branded stationery, and a printed schedule for the day. Simple gestures, but they made the whole thing feel real in a way it hadn't until that moment.

The Team — First Faces, First Names

My reporting manager came down to the lobby personally to receive me, which was a warm surprise. We took the elevator up to the floor and I got my first real look at the workspace. Open plan, natural light flooding in through floor-to-ceiling windows, clusters of desks arranged around collaborative zones. There were quiet pods along the edges for focused work, and a larger meeting room with glass walls at one end.

Introductions happened organically — a few people paused from their work to say hello, others gave a friendly nod. There's a peculiar social dance on Day One: you're trying to remember ten names simultaneously while also forming first impressions, reading the room, and pretending you remember which bay your desk is in.

What struck me most was how approachable everyone was. No one made me feel like the outsider — if anything, people went out of their way to include me in small moments: a quick explanation of where the printer is, which canteen floor has the best South Indian food, and the unofficial rule that the window-side desks are claimed by 9:15 AM.

The Office — Small Details That Tell a Big Story

Great offices reveal their culture through small things. And Tower D had plenty of those small things to notice. The whiteboards were covered in diagrams from yesterday's sessions, not erased — signalling that work here is a continuous conversation, not a series of isolated meetings. The plants were real, not plastic. Someone had left a half-read book on the common table. These aren't accidents; they're culture, accumulated quietly over time.

The cafeteria deserves its own paragraph. Generous seating, natural light, a proper South Indian counter, and a filtered coffee machine that produced something genuinely drinkable. I had my first cup of office coffee standing by the window, looking out over a sliver of Bangalore's skyline, and thought: I could get used to this.

The Afternoon — Getting Down to Work

After lunch, the tempo shifted. I sat in on a team sync — my first real look at how this team operates. The energy was focused without being tense. People challenged ideas without making it personal. There was a comfort with ambiguity that I found refreshing; not everything needed to be decided immediately, and that was considered a feature, not a bug.

My manager walked me through the current sprint, the backlog, the tools stack. There's a learning curve ahead — there always is in a new environment — but it felt navigable. More than that, it felt interesting.

What I Took Away

By the time I walked back out through the Tower D lobby at the end of the day, I was carrying more than just my bag. I was carrying a small but solid sense of belonging — the kind that doesn't arrive fully formed, but begins with a first day done right.

First days are strange creatures. They're simultaneously the most and least representative day of a new chapter. Everything is heightened — the details more vivid, the emotions closer to the surface. And yet, they're also artificial. The real texture of a workplace emerges over weeks and months, not hours.

But I'll say this: if Day One is any indication, the ETV JPMC office in Bangalore, Tower D, is going to be a place worth showing up for. The commute through this city, the view from that window, the people who took a moment to welcome a stranger — it all adds up to something that felt, quietly and genuinely, like the beginning of something good.

📍 Location: ETV JPMC Office, Tower D, Bangalore — one of the city's well-known corporate campuses, tucked into the heart of the tech corridor.

Looking Ahead

Tomorrow I'll remember more names. The day after, I'll stop double-checking which floor I'm on. A week from now, I'll have my own small rituals — my preferred desk, my coffee order, the colleagues I naturally gravitate toward. That's how belonging is built: not in a day, but day by day.

For now, Day One is in the books. And it was a good one.
