my trip to home town Palampur

Going back to where it all began — the misty valleys, familiar faces, and the quiet comfort of a place that has always felt like home.

Sanjay Dhiman • April 17, 2026 • 7 min read

There is a particular kind of travel that no guidebook can prepare you for — the kind where the destination is not a new place but an old one. Going back to your hometown is not the same as going somewhere for the first time. The novelty is gone, replaced by something harder to name: a mix of recognition and distance, of belonging and the odd sense that you have outgrown the place even as it has continued perfectly well without you.

I grew up in Palampur. Not visited it, not spent summers there — grew up there, in the fullest sense. The sound of rain on the tin roof of our house on a monsoon night, the walk to school through tea gardens that turned silver in the morning light, the particular smell of the town after a hailstorm. These are not memories I carry so much as textures that are simply part of how I experience the world. This spring, after longer than I want to admit, I went back.

The Drive In

I took the overnight bus from Delhi to Dharamsala — the old way, the way I used to as a student coming home for holidays. The early morning descent into the Kangra Valley, somewhere around 5 AM when the light is still deciding what it wants to be, is something I had forgotten and then remembered all at once. The valley opened below like a palm, and the Dhauladhar was there above it, white and enormous and exactly as I had left it.

A shared taxi from Dharamsala dropped me at the Palampur bus stand an hour later. I stood there for a moment longer than necessary. The chai stall on the corner was still there. The same hand-painted sign, or one that looked exactly like it. I ordered a cup and did not move for a while.

The Town That Waited

Palampur has changed, but carefully. The main market has expanded, there are more cafés and more tourists, the roads are wider in places. But underneath all of this the town has held its character — that particular unhurried quality that I have not found in quite the same form anywhere else. People still stop in the middle of conversations to watch the clouds move across the ridge. The pace of life here is calibrated to something other than productivity.

Something I noticed: In the years I have lived in bigger cities, I have unconsciously adopted a defensive kind of attention — always scanning, always slightly braced. In Palampur, that fell away within a few hours. I am not sure whether that is the place or just the relief of being somewhere I do not have to explain myself.

I walked the old route from the bus stand to our house — a route I must have walked a thousand times, so automatic that my feet remembered it before my brain did. Past the post office, left at the old dharamshala, up the lane where the bamboo grows so thick it makes a tunnel. The house looked smaller than I remembered, as childhood homes always do, but the garden was exactly right: chaotic, overflowing, my mother's project and her pride.

The Tea Gardens, Again and Differently

I have walked the tea gardens of Palampur many times in my life, in every season and at every age. Walking them now was a different experience — not because the gardens had changed, but because I had. As a child they were simply the backdrop of the town, as ordinary as pavement. As a teenager I found them boring. As a young adult I started to see them clearly for the first time. Now, in my thirties, I walked through them slowly and thought about how lucky I was to have grown up somewhere this beautiful without ever quite knowing it.

The first-flush tea was coming in. Workers moved along the rows in the early morning, their baskets filling steadily. I watched from the edge of the path, and one of the women recognized me — or at least recognized that I was looking at the gardens the way someone who belongs here looks, rather than the way a visitor does. She said something in Pahari that I caught only half of, but the tone was welcoming. I smiled back. Some things translate without translation.

Conversations with People Who Knew Me When

Coming home means encountering a version of yourself that others have preserved more faithfully than you have. My old neighbor Ramesh uncle still refers to me by the nickname I had at age seven. My school friend Vikram met me at a dhaba on the first evening and spent an hour reminding me of things I had genuinely forgotten — the time we missed the school bus deliberately to watch the cricket on a TV in a shop window, the argument I had with a teacher about a history test, the exact sequence of events of a monsoon that flooded the lower market.

These conversations are humbling in the best possible way. They remind you that your own account of your life is partial, that other people have been keeping notes you did not know about, that you exist in memory beyond your own.

Sitting with the Mountains

On my last evening I climbed to the flat rock above the house where, as a teenager, I used to go to be alone. It is a twenty-minute walk through pine and deodar, up a slope that is steep enough to make you think about your breathing. The rock is large enough to sit on comfortably and looks directly north at the Dhauladhar.

I sat there for two hours. The light changed — that slow Himalayan afternoon light that moves across the snow like a hand across a map. I did not read or listen to anything. I just sat with the mountains and let them be what they have always been: enormous, indifferent, and somehow, because of that very indifference, deeply comforting.

What Going Home Teaches You

I have been thinking since I left about what it means to go back to a place like this. I think it is partly about taking stock — measuring how far you have travelled, not just in distance but in the sense of who you have become. And it is partly about remembering that the person you were before all the becoming is still there, preserved in the places and the people who knew you then.

Palampur will keep being what it is whether I come back or not. The tea gardens will flush in March, the Dhauladhar will gather snow in November, the chai stall at the bus stand will open every morning at six. That continuity is, I think, the point. Some places hold still so that you have something to measure yourself against.

I will go back again. Sooner this time.
