Artificial Intelligence

How AI changed my day to day life — a personal account of living and working with intelligent tools.

Sanjay Dhiman • April 17, 2026 • 7 min read

I didn't notice it happening all at once. There was no single morning I woke up and decided to "adopt AI." It was more like water finding cracks — AI seeped into one routine, then another, then another, until I looked up one day and realised almost every part of my professional and personal life had a quiet intelligence running alongside it.

This is not a breathless tech manifesto. It's an honest account of what actually changed, what genuinely improved, what took adjustment, and where I still prefer being human about things.

The Morning Starts Differently Now

A few years ago, my morning routine involved scrolling through emails, Slack notifications, and news feeds — spending the first thirty minutes of the day in reactive mode before writing a single line of code or making a single decision. It was exhausting before the real work began.

Now I start with an AI-assisted brief. I have a lightweight daily prompt setup that pulls my calendar, open GitHub issues, and pending PR reviews, then asks an LLM to summarise what genuinely needs my attention today versus what can wait. It takes about ninety seconds. I go from zero to focused without the cognitive noise of manually triaging everything myself.

What surprised me: The biggest gain wasn't speed — it was arriving at deep work thirty minutes earlier each morning. Compounded over a year, that's roughly 180 extra hours of focused engineering time.

I also use AI to draft my daily standup notes. Not to replace thought, but to structure it — I speak out my thoughts naturally, and the draft organises them into something coherent. I still edit it, but the blank page problem is gone.

How My Engineering Workflow Changed

As a software engineer, the place I feel AI's impact most concretely is in the code-writing loop. I want to be precise about what changed and what didn't.

What genuinely improved

  • Boilerplate and scaffolding: Setting up a new Go service, writing Kubernetes YAML manifests, configuring Terraform modules — tasks that once took an hour of template hunting now take minutes. I describe the shape of what I need and review/adjust the output.
  • Debugging with context: Pasting an error trace and getting a structured explanation of root cause hypotheses, ranked by likelihood, is genuinely faster than cold-reading a stack trace alone — especially in unfamiliar codebases.
  • Writing tests: Generating unit test skeletons from a function signature is one of the highest-value uses I've found. I still write the meaningful edge cases myself, but the mechanical setup is handled.
  • Documentation: I write documentation far more consistently now because the activation energy dropped. Describe the function; get a first-draft docstring; refine it. Previously I would defer docs until "later" — which meant never.

What didn't change

  • System design and architecture decisions still require slow, careful human thought. AI can surface trade-offs I might have missed, but I don't trust it to make the call.
  • Code review still requires deep contextual understanding of the codebase history, team conventions, and long-term implications — things that live in human memory, not a prompt window.
  • The moments of genuine creative problem-solving — finding an elegant solution to a hard distributed systems problem — those still feel entirely mine.

Learning Accelerated — But Differently

I've been a continuous learner my whole career — books, courses, conference talks. AI changed the texture of learning more than the volume of it.

The most significant shift: I can now learn in the middle of doing. When I encounter an unfamiliar Kubernetes admission controller or an obscure Kafka configuration parameter, I don't stop work to read a blog post or documentation page. I ask a contextual question, get a focused explanation with a concrete example, and continue. The context-switching cost of "I need to go learn this before I can proceed" dropped dramatically.

It also changed how I approach new domains. When I was learning Apache Flink for our analytics pipeline, I used an AI as a live Socratic tutor — not to get answers, but to get better questions. "What are the failure modes of Flink checkpointing in high-throughput scenarios?" generates a richer learning conversation than any search query I would have written.

The distinction that matters: AI accelerated the retrieval phase of learning — getting facts, syntax, and examples faster. The integration phase — connecting new knowledge to existing mental models, building real intuition — that still requires time, reflection, and practice. AI didn't compress that.

Writing Changed the Most

I write a lot — technical documentation, design proposals, system design documents, blog posts. Writing was always where I felt the most friction because the gap between knowing something and articulating it clearly is real and uncomfortable.

AI didn't make me a better writer by writing for me. It made me a better writer by compressing the iteration cycle. I now write rough first drafts faster because I'm not trying to make them good — I'm just getting thoughts out, knowing I can quickly improve structure, clarity, and tone in the next pass with assistance. The internal editor that used to slow my first drafts down got quieter.

For technical documents like system design specifications, I now treat AI as a second reviewer: "What have I not addressed that a senior architect would ask?" The gaps it surfaces are usually real gaps, not hallucinations — because the questions it generates are structurally sound even when the specific content isn't authoritative.

The Personal Side — Where I Was More Cautious

I deliberately kept AI out of certain parts of my life, and I think this was the right call.

I don't use AI for personal journaling or processing difficult emotions. That's space where I want friction and slowness — where the act of struggling to articulate something is itself the point. I don't want efficiency there.

I also don't use it to make interpersonal decisions or draft sensitive personal messages. The relational context that matters in those situations — the history, the nuance, the tone that comes from knowing someone — that context can't be compressed into a prompt, and the cost of getting it wrong is too high.

There's also the question of over-reliance. I noticed a period about a year in where I would reach for AI for problems I should have been wrestling with myself — design decisions, debugging approaches. I pulled back deliberately. The goal is augmentation, not replacement of the thinking I need to do to grow.

What I Tell People Who Ask

The framing I find most useful: AI tools are like having a very fast, very well-read collaborator who has no stake in the outcome and no memory of yesterday. That collaborator is extraordinarily useful for certain tasks and completely wrong for others.

The people I see getting the most from AI are the ones who maintained their own high standards for the output — who treat AI-generated content as a draft to be edited, not a final product to be shipped. The discipline of judgment didn't go away; if anything, it became more important.

And the people I see struggling are those who either refuse to engage at all, losing ground in productivity and speed, or those who engage without critical review, producing confident-sounding nonsense at scale.

The honest summary: AI saved me roughly two hours a day on tasks that were real but not the point. It gave me those hours back to spend on the work that actually requires me. That trade has been worth every adaptation it required.

Where I Think This Goes

I don't think the trajectory is toward AI doing more of my job. I think it's toward AI handling the mechanical surface of tasks so that the human part — judgment, creativity, taste, context — becomes more purely the job.

The engineers who thrive in that world won't be those who resisted the tools, or those who outsourced their thinking to them. They'll be the ones who stayed deliberate about which cognitive work is theirs to do and used everything else to protect and amplify it.

I'm still figuring that line out. But I'm more curious than anxious about it — which, honestly, feels like the right place to be.
